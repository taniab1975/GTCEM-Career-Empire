# EST Knowledge Reactor: Time Management Design

## Purpose
Redesign the `Time Management` strand inside `Knowledge Reactor` so it feels like a playable revision mission rather than a content panel.

This topic should help students:

- define time management in practical terms
- recognise the core processes involved in planning and prioritising tasks
- apply tools and techniques to workplace and student-life examples
- explain decisions clearly in EST-style language

## Core Learning Target
Students need to be able to:

- explain that time management involves planning, organising, prioritising, monitoring, and adjusting tasks to meet deadlines
- identify appropriate analogue and digital tools
- apply time management to realistic workplace and life situations
- explain why a chosen order or tool is effective
- connect time management to initiative where relevant

## What The Source Sheet Emphasises
The time-management sheet is less about memorising one definition and more about a process:

- plan what needs to be done
- estimate time
- organise tasks/resources/steps
- prioritise urgent and important work
- set goals and deadlines
- follow a schedule
- stay focused
- adjust when issues arise
- balance multiple tasks
- monitor progress

It also gives strong material for:

- analogue vs digital tool choices
- process vs tool distinction
- apprentice plumber case study
- balancing school/work/life advice scenario
- links to initiative

## Best Gameful Framing
Use a practical reactor/operations theme instead of a worksheet.

Working title:

`Time Management Control Room`

Fantasy:

- the player is managing a live schedule board
- tasks, deadlines, and disruptions are entering the system
- the player must triage, sequence, adapt, and justify decisions
- each successful chamber stabilises the schedule and improves readiness

This fits the broader EST mission language better than inventing a completely separate theme.

## Topic Arc
This strand should feel like:

1. identify what matters
2. decide what happens first
3. adapt when conditions change
4. explain the logic in EST language

That turns the topic into a mini-story:

- the day begins with competing tasks
- the player builds a workable plan
- the day gets disrupted
- the player rescues the schedule
- the player explains the decision-making clearly

## Recommended Four-Step Structure

### 1. Triage The Task List
Goal:

- identify which tasks are urgent, important, or lower priority

Mechanic:

- sort or tap-place task cards into the right order or bucket

Good content examples:

- urgent repair
- scheduled site work
- materials pick-up
- paperwork/logbook
- school deadline
- shift start time

Learning move:

- classification

Why it works:

- time management starts with seeing what matters first

### 2. Build The Best Order
Goal:

- create the most effective sequence for completing work

Mechanic:

- reorder steps into the strongest schedule

Good content examples:

- review schedule
- prepare tools/materials
- travel/group tasks efficiently
- complete urgent work first
- do paperwork later

Learning move:

- sequencing

Why it works:

- students practise the logic of a good plan, not just naming skills

### 3. Rescue The Deadline
Goal:

- respond when the original plan is disrupted

Mechanic:

- scenario choice with immediate consequences/feedback

Good content examples:

- job takes longer than expected
- emergency job comes in
- traffic delays travel
- school/sport/work commitments clash

Learning move:

- application + adjustment

Why it works:

- this is where time management stops being static and becomes realistic

### 4. Upgrade The Answer
Goal:

- turn a weak response into a strong EST-style explanation

Mechanic:

- compare/repair writing
- finish a sentence or choose the stronger explanation

Strong sentence pattern:

- `This shows time management because the worker planned the task order, prioritised the urgent job, and adjusted the schedule when delays occurred.`

Learning move:

- justification + short-answer writing

Why it works:

- directly supports exam conditions

## Interaction Design Principles
To avoid becoming a polished worksheet, this strand should:

- show one clear problem at a time
- provide immediate feedback
- make wrong choices teach, not just punish
- keep each chamber short
- use visible progression
- let students retry without losing the best banked result

## What To Avoid

- too many tasks on screen at once
- long explanatory text before action
- asking students to simply read lists of tools
- making “analogue vs digital” a dry memory question
- repeating the same sort mechanic across all four steps

## Best Use Of The Content Sheet

### Most gameable content

- apprentice plumber workflow
- urgent vs scheduled work
- monitoring and adjusting
- digital and analogue tools
- life/work balance advice

### Best EST writing hooks

- “plan and prioritise”
- “meet specific deadlines”
- “monitor progress”
- “adjust when issues arise”
- “use tools such as calendars, lists, reminders, or schedules”

## Light Memory Supports
No chant needed.

Useful repeated language anchors:

- `Plan`
- `Prioritise`
- `Track`
- `Adjust`

These can appear subtly in chamber labels or microcopy, but should not dominate the design.

## Rubric Check

### Agency
Yes, if students must choose priorities, order, and responses.

### Failure teaches
Yes, if wrong order/response explains what consequence it creates.

### Narrative
Yes, the player is managing a live schedule under pressure.

### Progression
Yes, from triage to planning to rescue to explanation.

### Friction removal
Yes, if each chamber shows one problem clearly and uses short loops.

### Engaging without points?
Potentially yes, if the scenarios feel real and the sequencing logic is satisfying.

## Recommended Implementation Direction
The existing Time Management content in EST should be upgraded from a flat content bay into a custom arc, similar to what was done for Initiative.

Suggested mechanic family:

- custom `time-management-arc`

Suggested four internal steps:

- `triage`
- `sequence`
- `rescue`
- `write-upgrade`
